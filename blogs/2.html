Blog #2: Diversity in Africa

This week we read about some of the pre-European societies in Africa, the vast language diversity of Mozambique, and heard from a group of proffessors about their views on African diversity today. We did read a paper criticizing a book called Heart of Darkness by Joseph Conrad, though this was not directly about diversity.

I enjoyed listening to the panel of proffessors the most of this weeks assignments, getting a variety of first hand experiences helped show some of the bigger misconceptions the average Iowa State student might have about Africa.

Talking about Africa's diversity implies grouping Africa as a continent into a single entitiy. After this weeks reading, it seems impossible to generalize the continent that much. Not only is Africa massive, and there is great geographical diversity; but the countries themselves seem much more diverse than what would be typical in other parts of the world.

From the panel of proffessors, two were from Nigeria. Even though they were from the same country, they didn't speak the same language; Nigeria alone has over 250 languages. The food they ate, how they dressed, and the music they listened to, were different from eachother; even though they came from the same country.

This high level of diversity isn't unique to Nigeria, but seems to be common throughout Africa. Nigeria is unified through its use of English; so the majority of Nigerians can communicate even if their first languages are different. Africa as a whole does not have this; some countries use English, others French, and Portuguese.

One of the most interesting differences throughout Africa is in political structure. Some areas practice a patralineal method of choosing heirs, similar to how Europe does. The son of the current leader is next in line. But there are also have matralineal areas of Africa, where the successor is through the female line. The current ruler's sister's son would be the heir. This difference causes the power dynamics of gender to be quite different from patralineal areas.

During the ancient development of Africa, the saharah became a key economic landmark. When camels were introduced from the north, the saharah became traversable and opened new trade opportunities. This meant the areas surrounding the desert became economic powerhouses, due to them being an oasis for those travelling across the desert.

Along with camels being introduced from the north, the islamic religion was also spread from northern Africa down into the continent. When the Europeans came to the western coast, they spread christianity from there as well. This caused a heavy islamic presence in the north, and a similar christian presense in the west.
